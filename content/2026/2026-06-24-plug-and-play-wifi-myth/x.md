# Thread

1/ 🧵 "Plug-and-play" Wi-Fi is the most expensive lie in enterprise networking.

You don't plug it in. You engineer it — or you reopen the same ticket forever.

2/ The AP ships looking like an appliance. Mount it, power it, broadcast an SSID, done.

In an empty room, it works. The demo always works.

Then Monday shows up with 200 clients.

3/ The problem isn't the AP. It never is.

The AP is the one thing in wireless you actually control. What decides if Wi-Fi works lives outside the box.

4/ Walls. People. The breakroom microwave. The neighbor's APs bleeding through the floor.

And client radios that each make their own roaming decision and never ask your permission.

5/ Most "Wi-Fi is slow" tickets aren't the AP.

They're coverage holes, co-channel interference, or sticky clients clinging to a -80 dBm signal.

6/ The brownfield trap: every office that "fixed" coverage by adding more APs until the channels collided.

More radios in 2.4 GHz is more interference, not more capacity.

7/ Wired networks fail loudly. Wireless fails politely — a few users, some of the time, never on your dashboard.

Plug-and-play sells the AP. Engineering keeps the Wi-Fi.

What's your most-blamed AP that was really a design problem?
