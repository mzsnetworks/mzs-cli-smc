"Plug-and-play" Wi-Fi is the most expensive lie in enterprise networking.

You don't plug it in. You engineer it — or you reopen the same ticket forever.

The access point ships in a box that looks like an appliance. Mount it, power it, broadcast an SSID, done. And in an empty room, it works. The demo always works.

Then Monday morning shows up with 200 clients, and the appliance illusion dies.

The problem isn't the AP. It never is. The AP is the one thing in wireless you actually control. Everything that decides whether Wi-Fi works lives outside the box — the walls, the people, the breakroom microwave, the neighbor's APs bleeding through the floor, and client radios that each roam on their own decision and never ask your permission.

Most "the Wi-Fi is slow" tickets aren't the AP. They're a coverage hole nobody surveyed for, co-channel interference from APs fighting each other, or sticky clients clinging to a -80 dBm signal because roaming is the client's choice, not yours.

Operationally, plug-and-play didn't remove the work. It hid where the work is. The site survey, the channel and power plan, the band steering, the roaming thresholds — the box does none of it. It just stops you noticing the work was skipped until users do.

This is the brownfield trap too. Every office that "fixed" coverage by adding more APs until the channels collided learned the same lesson: more radios in 2.4 GHz is more interference, not more capacity.

Wired networks fail loudly. Wireless networks fail politely — a few users, some of the time, never on your dashboard.

Plug-and-play sells the access point. Engineering keeps the Wi-Fi. The clients decide who was right.
