# Master — IoT Is the New Brownfield

**Idea:** ideas-2026-07-16 #1 (IoT, Observation). IoT is the new brownfield — ten-year liabilities backed by two-year vendor attention spans, inherited by the network team when the pilot team disbands.
**Preset:** Business (MZS company voice — "we").
**Spine:** hook (IoT = brownfield) → POV (inheritance, not innovation, is the operational reality) → data (none — judgment post) → judgment (run IoT onboarding as brownfield intake; ask the exit question at entry) → landing (greenfield/brownfield/pallet triad + question).

---

IoT is the new brownfield. Every "smart" device you onboard is a ten-year liability backed by a two-year vendor attention span — and the network team inherits it when the pilot team disbands.

Brownfield has always meant one thing: operating other people's decisions. A ten-year-old campus network is a sediment of choices nobody remembers making. IoT recreates that situation — except the sediment now arrives by the pallet, with a cheerful project name.

Watch how these devices actually enter a network. Facilities pilots smart lighting. Operations trials sensors. A vendor demos badge readers to whoever took the meeting. Eighteen months later the pilot team has dissolved, the project channel is archived — and four hundred devices are still on the network, still running the firmware they shipped with.

Then the timelines cross. The building runs those devices for a decade. The vendor's enthusiasm runs about two years: firmware updates slow, the cloud API gets "sunset," support becomes a knowledge-base article. The device doesn't stop working — that's the trap. It keeps working, unpatched and unowned, on your network. You can't update it, you can't justify replacing it, and you can't ignore it.

The fix is to treat IoT onboarding as brownfield intake, not innovation theater. Before anything gets an IP: an inventory entry, a named owner, a device-class segment, and an answer to the exit question — who owns this when the pilot ends, and what's the decommission plan? If nobody can answer, you're not deploying a device. You're adopting a liability with a radio.

We've spent years modernizing networks that predate us. The skill transfers perfectly — because a smart building is just a legacy environment that hasn't aged yet.

Greenfield is a design problem. Brownfield is an inheritance problem. IoT is inheritance at scale, arriving by the pallet.

What's the oldest device on your network still phoning home to a vendor who stopped answering?
