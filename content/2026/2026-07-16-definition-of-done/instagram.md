Your automation didn't fail. Your team never agreed on what "done" meant. 🏁

Ask three engineers when a switch is "done":
→ config pushed
→ ports up, passing traffic
→ in monitoring, documented, handed to ops

Same task. Three finish lines.

Automate it, and the script picks one — and silently ships the other two as incomplete. It runs green every time. Someone downstream still inherits an unfinished switch.

The tool worked perfectly. The outcome was still wrong.

Automation encodes a definition of done ⚙️ If the humans never agreed where the finish line is, the tool doesn't resolve it — it hardcodes one version and executes the rest as a gap.

Manual work hid this. A person quietly filled the gap. Automation doesn't improvise.

The fix isn't better code. It's a definition of done the whole team signed off on — written, boring, specific.

Automation is only as aligned as the team behind it. It executes whatever agreement — or disagreement — you already had.

Where does your team's "done" quietly disagree? 👇
