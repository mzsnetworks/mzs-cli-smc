Every script you automate is a liability you now own. We only ever count the asset.

Automating a task feels like pure win. You did the work once; now it runs itself. Close the ticket, move on. What nobody logs is what you just took on: a piece of software that has to be maintained, understood, and trusted for the rest of its life — or quietly turned into a hazard.

That's automation debt, and it's real debt. It accrues interest.

The interest shows up as the playbook that breaks every time the vendor changes an API. The script only one person understands, who's now a single point of failure. The pipeline everyone's afraid to touch, so changes route around it. The "temporary" automation from two years ago that's now load-bearing and undocumented.

Manual work has a floor. It's slow, but it degrades gracefully — a human notices when something's off. Unmaintained automation has a trapdoor. It runs confidently on stale assumptions and fails silently, at scale, in ways nobody's watching for. The blast radius of neglected automation is bigger than the toil it replaced.

Here's the uncomfortable math: automation you don't maintain isn't saving you time. It's deferring a bill. And like all debt, the longer you ignore it, the more it costs to pay down — until one day it's cheaper to rip it out than to understand what it does.

Mature teams budget for this. They retire automation that isn't earning its keep. They assign owners, not just authors. They treat a pipeline like a product with a lifecycle, not a trophy from a hackathon.

Automate less, but own what you automate. A small amount of well-maintained automation beats a graveyard of scripts nobody dares run.
