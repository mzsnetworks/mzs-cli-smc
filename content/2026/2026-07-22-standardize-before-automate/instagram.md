Automation is a multiplier. Point it at a mess and all you get is a faster mess. 🌀

We reach for automation to fix inconsistency. The irony: automation needs consistency to work.

It assumes a pattern and applies it at scale. Feed it an environment where every switch was configured by a different engineer on a different Tuesday, and it doesn't clean that up — it reproduces the chaos, faster and in more places.

Ten snowflakes were manageable. Ten thousand, generated by a script, is a new class of problem ⚙️

The mechanism: automation encodes assumptions. In an inconsistent environment, those assumptions are true for some devices and quietly false for others — and the script can't tell the difference. Now your outage isn't one switch. It's every switch the job touched.

Standardize, then automate. Not because it's virtuous — because automation without it doesn't reduce your variance, it industrializes it.

If it's not consistent enough for a human to reason about, it's not consistent enough to automate.

What inconsistency would you fix before automating around it? 👇
