Automation is a multiplier. Point it at a mess and all you get is a faster mess.

We reach for automation to fix inconsistency. The irony is that automation needs consistency to work. It assumes a pattern — same device naming, same config structure, same interface conventions — and applies it at scale. Feed it an environment where every switch was configured by a different engineer on a different Tuesday, and it doesn't clean that up. It faithfully reproduces the chaos, faster and in more places.

Ten snowflakes were a manageable problem. Ten thousand snowflakes, generated by a script, is a new class of problem.

This is the step everyone wants to skip. Standardization is unglamorous — naming conventions, config templates, a golden config, an agreed source of truth. None of it demos well. So teams jump straight to automation, point it at their brownfield reality, and are surprised when it makes things worse.

Here's the mechanism. Automation encodes assumptions. In a standardized environment, those assumptions hold everywhere, so it's safe to run broadly. In an inconsistent one, the assumptions are true for some devices and quietly false for others — and the script can't tell the difference. It applies the same confident change to a device it doesn't actually understand. Now your outage isn't one misconfigured switch. It's every switch the job touched.

The order matters. Standardize, then automate. Not because standardization is virtuous, but because automation without it doesn't reduce your variance — it industrializes it.

If your environment isn't consistent enough for a human to reason about, it isn't consistent enough to automate. The messy manual step you're trying to skip is doing the real work: making the environment legible in the first place.

Standardize the thing first. Then let automation scale the order, not the chaos.
