## Thread option

1/ Automation is a multiplier. Point it at a mess and all you get is a faster mess. 🧵

2/ We reach for automation to fix inconsistency. The irony: it needs consistency to work. It assumes a pattern and applies it at scale — and faithfully reproduces whatever chaos you point it at, faster and in more places.

3/ Ten snowflakes were manageable. Ten thousand, generated by a script, is a new class of problem.

4/ The mechanism: automation encodes assumptions. In an inconsistent environment they're true for some devices and quietly false for others — and the script can't tell. Your outage isn't one switch. It's every switch the job touched.

5/ Standardize, then automate. Not because it's virtuous — because automation without it doesn't reduce your variance, it industrializes it.

What would you standardize first?
