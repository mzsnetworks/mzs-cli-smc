# LinkedIn — publish-ready

You can't buy accountability. You keep trying to, one tool at a time.

Every incident review ends the same way: someone proposes a new platform. A better observability suite. A single pane of glass. Another integration that will finally "give us visibility." And six months later the next outage looks exactly like the last one.

Here's the operational reality: a toolchain distributes information. It does not distribute ownership. Those are different problems, and only one of them is solved by a purchase order.

When every team has its own dashboard and all of them are green, an outage becomes an orphan. The network team sees healthy links. The platform team sees healthy pods. The app team sees healthy nodes. Everyone is right, everyone is green, and nobody owns the thing that's actually down — the service the customer is staring at.

That's not a tooling gap. That's an accountability gap wearing a tooling costume.

More tools usually make it worse. Each new pane of glass is one more place to point when something breaks, one more "well, my dashboard was fine." Visibility went up; ownership went down. You didn't buy clarity — you bought somewhere new to hide.

What actually moves the number is unglamorous and un-purchasable: someone owns the service end to end. One clear on-call path. A workflow that says who decides, not just what's red. Tools support that. They never replace it.

A toolchain tells you what is broken.
It never tells you whose job it is to fix it.
That line you draw yourself.

So before the next tool goes in the stack, ask the harder question: are we missing visibility — or is nobody actually on the hook?
