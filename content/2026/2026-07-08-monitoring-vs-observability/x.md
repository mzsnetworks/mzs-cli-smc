## Thread

1/ 🧵 Your monitoring stack is a museum.

It shows you what broke — beautifully, across 40 dashboards — then tells you nothing you can act on at 2am.

Monitoring answers *what*. Observability answers *why*. Most teams bought the first and renamed it the second.

2/ Monitoring is the questions you knew to ask in advance.

CPU. Interface errors. BGP up/down. A latency threshold.

You defined the check — so it can only warn you about the failure you already imagined.

3/ Observability is asking a question you *didn't* pre-plan — mid-outage, without shipping a new sensor.

"Every flow that touched this VLAN in 10 min, grouped by the firewall that dropped it."

Can't answer on the spot? You don't have observability. You have graphs.

4/ The tell is the war room.

Half of it spent SSH-ing into boxes for context the dashboards never captured? Those were monitoring.

The green wall said the parts were up — while the service was down.

5/ It's not a tooling purchase. A fifth pane of glass just moves the SSH session to a new tab.

It's instrumentation discipline: capture enough context that the next question is answerable before you know to ask it.

6/ Monitoring tells you the system is down.
Observability tells you why.
Engineering judgment is knowing which one you actually bought.

Which one is your team running — honestly?
