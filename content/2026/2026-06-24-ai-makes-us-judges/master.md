# LinkedIn Post — The Future of Network Engineering

I've watched the industry argue about NetOps vs. NetDevOps for a decade. We never settled it. The network stopped waiting.

Here's what I've concluded after building in this space: the labels describe the same migration through different doors.

NetOps walks in carrying "operations" and learns to automate.
NetDevOps walks in carrying "software development" and learns the network.
One protects the box. The other treats the box as code.

Same destination. They just argue about which suitcase to pack.

The terminology was always a proxy for the harder question I care about: what is a network engineer when the CLI is no longer the job?

The data answers it.

Gartner expects generative AI to write 25% of initial network configurations by 2027 — up from under 3% in 2024.
70% of operations staff will lean on AI for Day-2 management.
Automation adoption is climbing from ~44% to ~65% in two years.

The 2am, memorize-the-syntax, type-it-perfectly engineer isn't being promoted. That engineer is being abstracted.

So the future isn't NetOps or NetDevOps. It's this:

The value moves up the stack — from typing commands to designing the system that types them. Your edge stops being "I know the syntax" and becomes "I know what correct looks like, I can express it as code, and I can tell when the AI is confidently wrong."

That last part is the whole game. When a model generates a quarter of your configs, the rare skill isn't generation. It's judgment. Reading an AI-suggested change and knowing it'll blackhole half your traffic. That's not a junior task. It's the most senior task there is.

The engineers who win the next five years won't be the ones who resisted the shift, or the ones who blindly trusted the automation. They'll be the ones who became the reviewers, architects, and guardrails for a network that increasingly runs itself.

The CLI made us operators.
Code made us engineers.
AI is about to make us judges.

When the machine writes the config — are you the one who can tell if it's right?
