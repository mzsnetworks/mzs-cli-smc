You don't have an alerting problem. You have a nobody-trusts-the-alerts problem. 🔕

"Alert fatigue" blames the human — too many alerts, poor tired engineer. But engineers don't ignore alerts because they're tired. They ignore them because experience taught them to: the CPU alert that clears itself, the disk warning firing daily since March. Ignoring became the correct move. That's learning, not fatigue.

Every false positive withdraws trust. When the account hits zero, your real alerting system becomes informal: muted channels, mental lists, the engineer who just knows. Unwritten — and it leaves when they do.

The fix isn't fewer alerts. It's honest ones: every alert names an action and an owner. If firing changes nothing in the next ten minutes, it's telemetry wearing a pager. 📟

Noise trains people to ignore. Ignoring trains incidents to grow. Trust is the only alerting metric that matters.

How many of your alerts would on-call bet a 2am wake-up on? 👇
